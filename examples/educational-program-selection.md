Example 1 — Educational Program Selection
Decision Type: Undergraduate STEM program selection Framework Version: 2.7 Investment Tier: Elite — high-consequence, trajectory-defining input with no realistic reversal once committed Inputs Evaluated: 5 options — Electrical Engineering, Electrical Engineering Technology, Computer Science, Mathematics, Physics Hard Gates Applied: 3 Options Eliminated at Hard Gate: 1 Options Proceeding to Scoring: 4

Pre-Stage: Investment Classification
This decision is classified Elite. Undergraduate program selection is a four-year commitment with significant financial cost, direct impact on career entry point, and limited reversibility once enrolled. The output of this decision materially changes trajectory in ways that compound over time. Maximum analytical investment is justified.

Math eliminated — does not include applied technical coursework aligned with stated goal of working in technology product development.
Physics eliminated — same reason.
EET note: Failed HG-1 — EET does not lead to Professional Engineer licensure eligibility in most states. Proceeds to scoring with risk flag active. Human review required on this gate before final decision.
Three options proceed to full scoring: EE, EET, CS.

Evaluator Probability Statement
A knowledgeable advisor reviewing these five options against the stated goal of working at technology product companies — Apple, Google, consumer electronics — would immediately route toward EE or CS. EET's title contains the word "technology" which creates a surface-level match, but the curriculum and career outcome data diverge significantly from the stated goal. Math and Physics are eliminated cleanly. The surface signal risk on EET is high — a decision-maker without the right information is likely to select it for the wrong reason.

Gap Ramp Time Notes
EE gap — workload manageability scored 65. Not a hard requirement gap. Ramp tier: Weeks — study habit adjustment and resource identification closeable before first semester. Evaluation survivability: Yes.
EET gap — alignment with target industry scored 45. This is a fundamental curriculum mismatch, not a closeable gap. Ramp tier: Different Domain — EET prepares graduates for technician and field engineering roles, not software or systems engineering at technology product companies. Evaluation survivability: No. This gap compounds over time and is not addressable through effort.

Risk Flags
EE: Workload intensity is the primary risk. No fundamental mismatch with stated goal. Risk flag: manageable.
EET: Three compounding risk flags. Failed HG-1 on licensure eligibility. Fundamental curriculum misalignment with stated goal — scored 45 on target industry alignment. Career outcome data does not support placement at target company types. Risk flag: critical. This option passes on surface signal only.
CS: No significant risk flags. Strong alignment across all weighted categories. Minor workload note — no material concern.

Recommendation
CS — Tier 1, High Confidence with EE as an equally valid alternative.
CS leads on weighted total by a narrow margin, driven primarily by stronger career outcome data for software-focused technology product companies. EE is an equally strong choice and may be preferred if hardware-software boundary work or systems engineering is a stated interest. Both are defensible. EET is eliminated — its weighted score and risk profile are not competitive with either alternative, and its surface-level title match does not reflect actual curriculum or career outcome alignment.
Critical note: EET's elimination is not visible from the program title alone. A decision-maker relying on surface signals without a structured framework is at significant risk of selecting EET for the wrong reason. This is the exact failure mode SDF is designed to prevent.

Human Validation Notes
Recommendation accepted. CS or EE selected — final choice between the two delegated to stated interest in hardware vs software emphasis.
Framework iteration noted: HG-1 on licensure eligibility should be reviewed for relevance if the decision-maker's stated goal is technology product development rather than traditional engineering practice. Licensure may be less relevant than initially weighted for this specific goal. Flagged for v-next rule iteration.
