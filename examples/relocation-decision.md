
Example 2 — Relocation Decision
Decision Type: City relocation evaluation Framework Version: 2.7 Investment Tier: Elite — high-consequence, high-friction decision with significant financial and lifestyle impact Inputs Evaluated: 3 options — Colorado Springs, CO | Denver, CO | Dallas, TX Hard Gates Applied: 3 Options Eliminated at Hard Gate: 0 Options Proceeding to Scoring: 3

Pre-Stage: Investment Classification:
This decision is classified Elite. Relocation involves financial cost, social disruption, and commitment to a new market and lifestyle context. The output materially changes trajectory across compensation potential, professional network, and quality of life simultaneously. Maximum analytical investment is justified.

No options eliminated. Denver flagged on HG-2 — cost of living increase relative to Colorado Springs requires compensation improvement to maintain equivalent standard of living. Proceeds with risk flag active.

Evaluator Probability Statement:
A knowledgeable advisor reviewing these three options for a candidate targeting commercial technology roles would immediately note that Colorado Springs' job market is heavily weighted toward defense and government contracting — the dominant local employer type actively works against commercial market positioning. Denver resolves this partially with a growing commercial tech presence but introduces cost of living friction. Dallas has the strongest commercial tech density of the three and the most direct access to target company types — AMD, Apple, Tesla, and a dense commercial tech ecosystem — but requires the highest relocation investment and the greatest lifestyle disruption.

Gap Ramp Time Notes:
Colorado Springs gap — commercial job market density scored 45. Ramp tier: Months to Different Domain. The market composition is structurally weighted toward defense contracting. This is not a gap closeable through effort — it is a market characteristic that requires a location change to address. Evaluation survivability for commercial target roles from Colorado Springs: limited without remote positioning.
Denver gap — cost of living vs compensation ratio scored 65. Ramp tier: Weeks to Months. Closeable if compensation improves to target range within the standard hiring cycle. If compensation does not move above current baseline, cost of living friction becomes a sustained pressure point. Monitor closely.
Dallas gap — proximity to existing network scored 30. Ramp tier: Months. Network rebuilding is a real cost but addressable through deliberate effort over 6–12 months. Does not affect job market access or compensation ceiling. Manageable.

Risk Flags:
Colorado Springs: Structural market mismatch with commercial target domain. Defense market will continue to pull toward cleared roles regardless of resume positioning. Risk flag: significant. Not a temporary condition.
Denver: Cost of living friction if compensation does not move. Otherwise no fundamental misalignment with stated goal. Risk flag: conditional — monitor compensation trajectory.
Dallas: Network disruption and relocation cost are real but finite. No structural misalignment with stated goal. Strong commercial tech presence with AMD, Apple, and Tesla all having significant Dallas-area operations. Risk flag: manageable.

Colorado Springs conflicts across three of four trajectory dimensions. It solves the immediate stability problem but actively works against the stated commercial positioning goal over time. Denver accelerates on most dimensions with one neutral. Dallas accelerates across all four.

Recommendation:
Dallas, TX — Tier 1, High Confidence for maximum trajectory acceleration toward commercial technology roles.
Denver, CO — Tier 2, Acceptable if relocation friction or financial constraints make Dallas impractical in the near term. Strong intermediate move that meaningfully improves positioning over Colorado Springs without requiring full lifestyle disruption.
Colorado Springs, CO — Proceed with Caution. Acceptable as a temporary holding position while pipeline develops and financial pressure is managed. Not a viable long-term position for stated commercial goals — the structural market composition actively works against the goal regardless of resume quality or application volume.
Decision note: If financial pressure requires near-term stability, Colorado Springs with active pipeline development toward Denver or Dallas is the correct sequencing. The framework recommends Dallas as the highest-trajectory outcome but acknowledges that constraint timing affects when that move is executable, not whether it is correct.
