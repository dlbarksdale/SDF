# CDF 1.0 — Career Diagnostic Framework
### Framework Document

---

## Purpose

CDF produces six diagnostic outputs from a cold resume read. No setup required. No baseline variables. No category routing. The framework runs on whatever you provide and scales precision to the amount of context given.

This is the working document. Upload your resume — and a job description and additional context if you have them — then run through each section in order.

---

## Input Classification

Before running any section, classify what you have provided. This determines which sections run and how precise the outputs will be.

| Input Level | What You Provided | Sections Available |
|---|---|---|
| Level 1 | Resume only | Sections 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 |
| Level 2 | Resume + job description | Sections 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 + Skills Match + Risk + Preliminary Verdict |
| Level 3 | Resume + job description + context | All sections at maximum precision |

**Context** means any combination of: current base salary, current location, target location or remote preference, target domain or role type, credentials not visible on the resume — clearances, certifications, independent projects.

The more context provided the tighter the salary estimates and the more specific the best path recommendations. At Level 1 every output is directional. At Level 3 every output is actionable.

---

## Section 1 — Current State Read

Read the resume as submitted. Assess what the market actually sees — not what the candidate intended to communicate.

Output the following:

**Implied Domain**
What field or industry does this resume suggest the candidate works in? Be specific. Not "technology" — "backend distributed systems engineering" or "consumer product mechanical design" or "financial compliance analysis."

**Implied Seniority**
What level does the resume communicate — junior, mid, senior, staff, principal? Base this on scope of work described, not years of experience or title alone.

**Implied Specialization**
What specific technical or functional specialty does the work history reveal? This is the push button get banana moment — what the candidate actually is versus what they think they are. Look across all roles for the pattern that the candidate may not have named explicitly. A candidate who has spent five years doing something specific rarely describes it that specifically because they're too close to it to see it from the outside. Name it.

**Implied Market Value**
Based on the implied domain, seniority, and specialization — what is the realistic market value range for this profile as currently presented? State a range, not a single number. Note whether this is a national estimate or market-specific based on information provided.

**Positioning Gaps**
What is the candidate leaving on the table based on the resume as submitted? Where is the framing weak, the vocabulary wrong for the target audience, or the most valuable credential buried or absent entirely?

---

## Section 2 — Resume Improvement Guide

Provide specific, actionable changes to the resume as submitted. This section always runs in the following order:

**Title Reframe — Run First**

For every role on the resume, assess whether the listed title accurately reflects the specialization the job description reveals. Suggest a retitled version where the current title undersells the actual work performed.

The employer's internal title is a compensation band designation. The resume title is a market positioning decision. They are not required to be the same thing.

A Contract Software Engineer whose description reads Full Stack Software Engineer (.NET, React) should be titled Full Stack Software Engineer (.NET, React). A Mechanical Engineer whose work reveals years of designing and iterating consumer product enclosures for mass manufacturing should be titled Product Mechanical Engineer or Consumer Hardware Engineer. A Project Manager whose scope reveals Technical Program Management should say so. The work defines the title. The employer's label does not.

Output format for each role:

*Current title: [as listed]*
*Suggested title: [retitled version]*
*Reason: [one sentence on what the description reveals that the current title doesn't capture]*

---

**Specialty Surfacing**

Identify the technical or functional specialty identified in Section 1 and provide specific bullet rewrites or additions that make it explicitly visible rather than implied. The specialty should be readable in the first third of the resume without inference.

**Vocabulary Translation**

Identify language on the resume that is written for the wrong audience. Defense and government vocabulary on a commercial-facing resume. Generic descriptions where specific technical terms would signal domain depth. Vague scope claims where concrete metrics or system descriptions would be more credible. Provide specific rewrites.

**Lead Credential Identification**

Identify the single credential on the resume that most changes how the entire profile reads when surfaced correctly — and confirm whether it is currently visible in the first third of the resume. If it isn't, provide specific guidance on reordering or reframing to move it forward.

**Independent Project or Portfolio**

If an independent project, open-source contribution, or portfolio work is present on the resume — assess whether it is framed as primary evidence or buried as a side note. If it's buried, provide specific reframing guidance. If it's absent entirely, note that its absence is a gap that targeted candidates in this domain are filling and that it represents one of the highest-leverage additions available.

---

## Section 3 — Market Positioning Projection

Project what the profile looks like after the recommended changes in Section 2 are applied. Do not project aspirationally — project based on what the evidence supports.

**Before State**
Summarize the market positioning of the resume as submitted in two to three sentences. What roles is this resume competitive for today. What market. What compensation range.

**After State**
Summarize the market positioning of the resume with Section 2 changes applied. What roles does it become competitive for. What market. What compensation range. Be specific about the delta — this is the value of doing the work.

**Remote Viability**
Assess whether remote work is realistic for this profile in the current market. Be honest. Some profiles and markets support remote work at the candidate's target compensation level. Some don't. If remote is not realistic at the target compensation level in the current market, say so directly and explain what would need to change for it to become viable.

**Relocation Reality**
If the implied market or target market requires relocation to maximize the profile's value, name the markets specifically. Explain what the profile is worth in the current market versus what it would be worth in the target market. The delta is the financial case for the move.

---

## Section 4 — Best Path For Success

This section produces the honest recommendation most career tools refuse to give. The goal is accuracy, not comfort. Every recommendation must be specific and actionable — not directional encouragement.

Assess and output a recommendation for each of the following dimensions. If a dimension is not relevant to the profile as read, state N/A and move on.

**Market**
Is the candidate in the right geographic market for their profile and goals? If not, which market and why. What is the compensation delta between current market and optimal market.

**Remote vs Onsite**
Is remote realistic for this profile at the target compensation level. If not, what needs to change before it becomes realistic — a credential, a domain shift, a company tier change, a seniority level increase.

**Domain Alignment**
Is the candidate's current domain the highest-value application of their skills or are they in an adjacent domain that pays less for the same underlying capability. If a higher-value domain is accessible, name it and describe what the transition requires.

**Retooling Assessment**
Is the candidate behind the curve in their domain in a way that will cap their trajectory without deliberate intervention. If yes, what specifically needs to be added — a certification, a project, a skill, a framework — and what timeline is realistic for closing that gap.

**Credential Leverage**
Does the candidate have a credential — clearance, certification, independent project, domain-specific qualification — that is not currently being leveraged and that materially changes their market value when surfaced correctly. If yes, name it and explain how to surface it.

**Sequence**
Given all of the above — what is the correct order of operations. What should the candidate do first, second, and third to move from current state to optimal state as efficiently as possible.

---

## Section 5 — Current Market Value

State the candidate's current market value based on the resume as submitted.

**If current base salary was provided:**
Compare current salary to market value range. State clearly whether the candidate is being paid correctly, underpaid, or significantly underpaid. The gap between current salary and market value is the most actionable number in this entire diagnostic.

**If current base salary was not provided:**
Estimate market value range based on implied domain, seniority, specialization, and implied market. State the range and note the confidence level based on how much context was provided.

In both cases output:

*Current market value range: [low] — [high]*
*Confidence level: [Low / Medium / High] based on [context provided]*
*Key driver of range: [one sentence on what most affects placement within the range]*

## Section 6 — Salary Progression Map

Three waypoints calibrated to current salary. If current base salary was provided, waypoints are set above the current baseline at meaningful increments. If current base salary was not provided, default waypoints of $100K / $150K / $200K are used — if these are below your current salary, provide your base for a relevant progression map.

Waypoints are not raises. Each one represents a meaningfully different profile, market position, and capability level than the one before it. The actions required to reach each waypoint reflect that difference.

| Current Salary | Waypoint 1 | Waypoint 2 | Waypoint 3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Not provided or below $100K | $100K | $150K | $200K |
| $100K–$124K | $130K | $160K | $200K |
| $125K–$149K | $150K | $185K | $225K |
| $150K–$174K | $175K | $210K | $250K |
| $175K–$199K | $200K | $240K | $300K |
| $200K–$249K | $250K | $300K | $400K |
| $250K+ | $300K | $400K | $500K+ |

**Actions required:** Specific changes — resume retitling, specialty surfacing, credential addition, market change, domain shift, or some combination. Name each one.

**Timeline:** Realistic estimate based on current profile and gap size.

**Market or domain shift required:** Yes or No. If yes, name the market or domain specifically.

**Profile requirements:** What does the resume need to show to command this number in the relevant market.

**Actions required:** Specific changes required beyond Waypoint 1. Build on the prior waypoint — do not repeat it.

**Timeline:** Realistic estimate from current state, not from Waypoint 1.

**Market or domain shift required:** Yes or No. If yes, name it.

### Waypoint 3 — [Insert from table above based on current salary]

**Profile requirements:** What does the resume need to show to command this number in the relevant market.

**Actions required:** Specific changes required beyond Waypoint 2. This waypoint often requires a combination of domain shift, market change, seniority level increase, and a credential or project that signals exceptional depth. Name every component.

**Timeline:** Realistic estimate from current state. Be honest — this waypoint may be 3–5 years away for some profiles. That's not a failure. That's a plan.

**Market or domain shift required:** Yes or No. If yes, name it.

> ⚠️ **Important Note on Salary Estimates**
>
> The salary figures in this section are estimates based on limited information. A cold diagnostic has no visibility into your specific location, target market, cost of living, clearance status, depth of skills beyond how they're described on the resume, or the full context of your experience. These numbers are directional — useful for understanding roughly where you stand and what's possible, not precise enough to anchor a compensation negotiation.
>
> To get a meaningful salary analysis you need to provide: your current base salary, your current location, your target location or remote preference, your target domain and role type, and any credentials that add market value beyond what's on the resume — clearances, certifications, independent projects.
>
> The cold diagnostic shows you the shape of the opportunity. The full JAF setup shows you the number.

## Level 2 Addition — Skills Match & Preliminary Verdict

*Runs only when a job description is provided alongside the resume.*

**Cold Skills Match**

Evaluate the resume as submitted against the job description. Use the JAF 2.8 skills match methodology — hard requirements at full weight, preferred requirements two-sided, gap ramp time estimation across Days / Weeks / Months / Different Domain tiers.

Output the skills match table:

| Requirement | Match | Ramp Time | Survivability |
|---|---|---|---|
| [Hard requirement] | [Strong / Partial / Gap] | [— or ramp tier] | [— or survivability call] |

**Cold Fit Score**

Output a fit score based on the cold resume against the job description. This is not an optimized fit score — it reflects the resume as submitted with no category optimization and no evidence library consultation. A full JAF analysis on the same role with a targeted resume will produce a different and typically higher fit score.

*Cold fit score: X / 10*
*Note: This score reflects the resume as submitted. A targeted resume for this specific role and category would produce a more precise assessment.*

**Preliminary Verdict**

Output one of the four JAF verdicts based on the cold analysis:

- APPLY
- APPLY (Selective Effort)
- APPLY (Stretch but Defensible)
- DO NOT APPLY

Include one sentence of rationale. Note that this is a cold verdict — a full JAF analysis may produce a different result once the resume is optimized and baseline variables are set.

---

## What CDF Does Not Produce

CDF does not assign a resume category. It does not select or optimize a resume for a specific role. It does not run investment tier classification. It does not produce a compensation anchor calibrated to a specific band. It does not evaluate career trajectory impact against defined baseline variables. It does not consult an evidence library.

All of those require JAF. CDF is the diagnostic that tells you whether JAF is worth setting up and what lanes you should be running when you do.

---

## From CDF to JAF

Once CDF shows you what lanes you should be running and what your profile looks like with targeted changes, JAF gives you the structured evaluation framework to run every opportunity through with precision — fit scoring, risk assessment, compensation anchoring calibrated to your qualification level, trajectory impact, and a binary verdict with documented rationale.

CDF answers: where am I and where could I go.
JAF answers: should I take this specific opportunity and what should I ask for.
SDF is the architecture that makes both of them work.
